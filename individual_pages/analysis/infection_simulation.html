Infection Simulation
Updated

The following simulations are based on a simple model of infection, one that does not necessary represent the behaviour of the Coronavirus. The model is set up as such:

  • Immune individuals cannot become infected or transmit the virus.
  • Infected individuals only have one opportunity to infect surrounding individuals.
  • For an individual with position (x,y), the surrounding individuals are defined as those in the Cartesian product of the two sets {x-1, x, x+1} and {y-1, y, y+1}, excluding (x,y) and any coordinate outside of the grid.
  • Individuals can only become infected once.
  • Successfully vaccinated individuals have the same characteristics as immune individuals, unsuccessfully vaccinated individuals have the same charateristics as non-immune individuals.
  • The distribution of immune and non-immune individuals is random, as is the location of the individuals starting the simulation infected.
  • The simulation ends when there are no more newly infected individuals.

The simulations below have the following parameters:

  • 10% of individuals are naturally immune.
  • Vaccine efficacy is 90%.
  • 20%, 40%, 60%, and 80% of individuals are vaccinated, meaning an additional 0.9 times that number are immune.
  • The probability of an infected individual infecting a surrounding, non-immune, individual is 0.85.


The results in the above simulations can vary for any given level of immunity due to the random distribution of the infected and immune individuals, and the random nature of the infection. The following graphs summarise 1,000 simulations at each vaccination level using the same parameters as above and provide a couple of key insights:

  • The mean number of infections for each vaccination level falls as the proportion of vaccinated individuals increases.
  • The variation in the number of infections is greatest when the level of immunity – a combination of natural and vaccine-induced – is around 50%. The variation decreases as the level of immunity approaches both 0% and 100%. This random variation in infection levels can be a factor in explaining the differing levels of infection seen in countries were other factors associated with virus outbreak outcomes are similar.
